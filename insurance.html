Homeowner's Insurance

Actions required before, during, and after construction.

🔵 Pre-Construction
Notify your insurer before signing any contracts. Non-disclosure of a pool addition can result in denied claims or policy cancellation.
Confirm pool classification and coverage limit. Inground pools are typically “Other Structures,” capped at ~10% of your dwelling coverage. Verify this is sufficient and request an increase if not.
Review and increase liability limits. Standard policies carry $100K–$300K. Pool ownership warrants a minimum of $300K–$500K; $1M+ recommended. An umbrella policy is typically the most cost-effective path.
Confirm policy exclusions specific to pools. Diving boards, slides, and flood damage are commonly excluded. Get explicit answers in writing.
Verify contractor carries its own liability and workers’ comp. Obtain certificates of insurance (COIs) before work begins. Whether your homeowner policy responds to a contractor injury claim depends on specific Coverage E exclusion language and MA endorsements — confirm in writing with your carrier rather than assuming.
If contractor is a sole proprietor without workers’ comp, address the gap explicitly. MA law exempts sole props from carrying WC on themselves, but requires it for any employee or misclassified sub. Require written disclosure of every person working on the property and a WC COI or sole-prop exemption acknowledgment for each. Consider a signed waiver/indemnification in the contract.
Get written answers from your carrier on two specific questions: (1) Does Coverage E respond to a bodily injury claim by an independent contractor performing work under written agreement who is not eligible for WC benefits? (2) Does your umbrella’s WC exclusion bar coverage for a claim by a contractor or helper who was not covered by WC but arguably should have been under MA law?
Review Medical Payments to Others limit. Base ISO limit of $1,000 is low for a property with a pool and active construction traffic. Raising to $5,000–$10,000 is typically inexpensive and can prevent small incidents from escalating into liability claims.
Ask about builder’s risk / course-of-construction coverage. Confirm in writing whether Coverage A responds to accidental construction damage to the existing dwelling (equipment strike, fire, water intrusion during build), or whether a course-of-construction rider is needed. Most standard HO-3 forms exclude damage from construction operations.
🟠 During Construction
Verify active contractor COIs at each phase. Request updated certificates if subcontractors are brought on mid-project.
Document the site throughout construction. Dated photos and inspection records support any future damage claim and demonstrate due diligence.
Secure open excavations. An open dig is a liability exposure. Temporary fencing reduces risk and may be required by your insurer or Upton building code.
🟢 Post-Construction
Notify insurer that construction is complete. Provide final cost. Update “Other Structures” coverage limit to match replacement value if needed.
Confirm MA code-required fencing is in place. 780 CMR requires a 4-ft self-closing, self-latching barrier. Most insurers require this before providing liability coverage — failure to comply can void claims.
Install pool and gate alarms. Reduces liability exposure and may qualify for a small premium discount.
Finalize umbrella policy if not already in place. Confirm it explicitly covers pool-related incidents.
Update home’s insured replacement value. Pool and hardscape add to total property value; ensure dwelling and structures coverage reflects the increase.
Winterize annually and maintain the pool. Damage from neglect or failure to winterize is explicitly excluded from coverage by most policies.
⚠️ Insurance requirements vary by carrier and policy. Verify all items with your insurance agent. For MA-specific liability and attractive nuisance exposure, consider consulting a local attorney.
